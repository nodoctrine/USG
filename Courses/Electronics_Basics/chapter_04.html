4.1 DC vs AC & the Sinusoidal Waveform

Direct Current (DC) flows in one direction only. Its magnitude stays constant over time — a flat line on a graph. Batteries, regulated power supplies, and solar cells produce DC.

Alternating Current (AC) reverses direction periodically, producing a continuously rising and falling waveform. It is generated when a coil rotates inside a magnetic field, and the most common shape is a sine wave.

The general equation for an AC voltage is:

  • v(t) = Vm sin(ωt + φ)
  • Vm — peak (maximum) amplitude; the tallest point above the zero line
  • ω — angular frequency in rad/s; how fast the wave oscillates
  • t — time in seconds
  • φ — phase angle; shifts the waveform left or right in time without changing its shape

Frequency and period are related: f = 1/T (Hz) and ω = 2πf (rad/s). The US mains supply runs at 60 Hz, giving ω = 2π × 60 ≈ 377 rad/s.

Step 2 — Angular Frequency (ω)

The term ω inside the sine sets the oscillation speed:

  • ω = 2πf  →  higher f means larger ω
  • Period: T = 2π / ω
  • US mains: f = 60 Hz → ω = 377 rad/s

Example: v(t) = 100 sin(20t)
ω = 20 rad/s → f = 20 / (2π) ≈ 3.18 Hz → T = 1/3.18 ≈ 0.314 s
Step 3 — Phase Angle (φ)

Adding φ inside the sine shifts the waveform in time:

  • φ > 0 — waveform shifts left (reaches its peak earlier)
  • φ < 0 — waveform shifts right (peak arrives later)
  • φ does not change the amplitude or frequency

Example: v(t) = 100 sin(20t + π/2) has φ = π/2 = 90° — the waveform starts at its peak (like a cosine).
Step 4 — Reading the Full Equation

Given: v(t) = 100 sin(20t + π/2)

Vm= 100 V  (peak amplitude)
ω= 20 rad/s  →  f ≈ 3.18 Hz, T ≈ 0.314 s
φ= π/2 = 90°  (shifted left by a quarter cycle)
Vm sets the height — the maximum distance from the zero line to the peak.

4.2 Amplitude, RMS & Phase

The amplitude Vm is the peak value — the maximum distance from zero to the waveform's crest. Because a sine wave is symmetric, the average voltage over a full cycle is zero (positive and negative halves cancel), making the average useless for power calculations.

The RMS value (root mean square), also called the effective value, solves this. It represents the equivalent DC voltage that delivers the same power (heating effect) as the AC signal. For a pure sine wave:

  • Vrms = Vm / √2 = 0.707 × Vm
  • Equivalently, Vm = Vrms × √2 ≈ 1.414 × Vrms
  • The 120 V from a US wall outlet is an RMS value — its peak is about 170 V
  • Same relationship holds for current: Irms = Im / √2

The phase angle φ describes a time offset between two signals of the same frequency. If signal A peaks before signal B, A is leading B. If A peaks after, A is lagging B. Resistors introduce no phase shift; reactive components (inductors, capacitors) do.

Lecture 4.2.1 — Why RMS? The Power Equivalence ○
Step 1 — The Problem with Averaging AC

A 120 V sine wave swings from +170 V to −170 V. If you simply average all the instantaneous values over one cycle, the positive and negative halves cancel exactly — the average is zero.

Yet a 120 V AC supply clearly delivers power — your lights turn on. So the arithmetic average is not a useful measure of AC magnitude for power purposes.
Step 2 — The RMS Solution

Root Mean Square works in three steps:

  1. Square every instantaneous value (negatives become positive)
  2. Mean — average those squared values over one cycle
  3. Root — take the square root of that average

For a sine wave: the average of sin²(ωt) over a cycle = 1/2, so Vrms = Vm × √(1/2) = Vm / √2.
Step 3 — The Formula

Vrms = Vm / √2 ≈ 0.707 × Vm
US wall outlet example: Vrms = 120 V
Vm = 120 / 0.707 ≈ 170 V peak

This 170 V is what appears on an oscilloscope. The 120 V is what a standard AC voltmeter shows — and it equals the DC voltage that would produce the same heating in a resistor.
Step 4 — Power with RMS

Because Vrms is the DC-equivalent, all DC power formulas work directly:

FormulaUse
P = Vrms × IrmsAverage AC power
P = Vrms² / RResistive load
P = Irms² × RHeating in a resistor
The average of a full AC sine cycle is zero — the positive and negative halves cancel.

4.3 AC Resistance & Inductive Reactance

In DC circuits, resistance R is the only opposition to current. In AC circuits, the broader concept of impedance (Z) replaces resistance. Impedance has the same unit (ohms, Ω) and Z replaces R in Ohm's Law: V = I × Z.

Resistors in AC: A resistor behaves identically in AC and DC. Its impedance Z = R, voltage and current are perfectly in phase (0° difference), and resistance does not depend on frequency.

Inductors in AC: An inductor (coil of wire) stores energy in a magnetic field. Its opposition to AC is called inductive reactance:

  • XL = ωL = 2πfL (ohms)
  • XL is directly proportional to frequency — higher f means higher opposition
  • Voltage across the inductor leads the current by 90°
  • At DC (f = 0): XL = 0 — inductor acts like a short circuit (wire)
  • At very high frequency: XL → ∞ — inductor blocks current entirely

The imaginary unit j (= √−1) in jXL encodes the 90° phase shift. For magnitude calculations, |jXL| = XL.
Lecture 4.3.1 — Inductive Reactance & Frequency ○
Step 1 — Why Inductors Oppose AC

An inductor's voltage is v(t) = L × di/dt — it's proportional to how fast the current is changing. AC current changes constantly, so the inductor is always pushing back.

The higher the frequency, the faster the current tries to change — and the harder the inductor resists. This is why inductive reactance grows with frequency.
Step 2 — The XL Formula

XL = ωL = 2πfL  (ohms)
  • Double f → double XL
  • Double L → double XL
  • XL is measured in ohms Ω

Example: L = 100 mH, f = 50 Hz
XL = 2π × 50 × 0.1 = 10π ≈ 31.4 Ω
Step 3 — 90° Phase Lead

For an inductor with i(t) = Im sin(ωt):

v(t) = L di/dt = XL Im cos(ωt)
cosine = sine shifted left by 90°. So voltage reaches its peak 90° before the current does.

Memory aid: "ELI the ICE man" — E leads I in an inductor (L); I leads E in a capacitor (C).
Step 4 — Frequency Extremes

An inductor resists changes in current — the faster the current changes, the greater the opposition.

4.4 Series RL Circuit

When a resistor and an inductor are connected in series to an AC source, the total opposition is the impedance Z, which combines R (real part) and XL (imaginary part):

  • Z = R + jXL  —  complex form
  • |Z| = √(R² + XL²)  —  magnitude (ohms); used in Ohm's Law
  • I = V / |Z|  —  current magnitude
  • θ = tan−1(XL / R)  —  phase angle; how much voltage leads current

The impedance triangle is a right triangle where R is the horizontal leg, XL is the vertical leg, and |Z| is the hypotenuse. This is the same shape as the voltage phasor triangle (VR, VL, Vs).

Key behavior: in a series RL circuit, the current lags the source voltage. A larger XL (or larger L or higher f) produces a larger phase angle and more lag.

Lecture 4.4.1 — Analysing a Series RL Circuit ○
Step 1 — Identify Given Values

Example circuit: Vs = 200 V, ω = 500 rad/s, R = 60 Ω, L = 0.16 H

Before calculating, identify every known quantity:

Source voltageVs = 200 V
Angular frequencyω = 500 rad/s
ResistanceR = 60 Ω
InductanceL = 0.16 H
Step 2 — Find Inductive Reactance XL

XL = ω × L = 500 × 0.16 = 80 Ω

XL = 80 Ω means the inductor provides 80 ohms of opposition at this frequency. The resistor provides a constant 60 Ω. They cannot be added directly because they are 90° apart.
Step 3 — Find Impedance Magnitude |Z|

R and XL form two legs of a right triangle:

|Z| = √(60² + 80²) = √(3600 + 6400) = √10000 = 100 Ω

This is a classic 3–4–5 Pythagorean triple scaled by 20: 60 = 3×20, 80 = 4×20, 100 = 5×20.
Step 4 — Current & Phase Angle

I = Vs / |Z| = 200 / 100 = 2 A
θ = tan−1(XL / R) = tan−1(80 / 60) = tan−1(1.33) ≈ 53°

The current lags the supply voltage by 53°. Voltage across R = 2 × 60 = 120 V. Voltage across L = 2 × 80 = 160 V. Check: √(120² + 160²) = √40000 = 200 V ✓
