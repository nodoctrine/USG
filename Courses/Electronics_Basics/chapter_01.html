1.1 Electricity & Matter

Electricity is all around us — it powers lighting, tools, motors, and control systems. To understand how it works, we start with the building blocks of all matter: atoms.

Every atom has a central nucleus containing protons (positive charge) and neutrons (no charge). Orbiting the nucleus are electrons, which carry a negative charge. When electrons move together through a material, that flow of charge is what we call electric current.

Materials differ in how easily they let electrons move:

  • Conductors — loosely held outer electrons move freely (copper, silver, gold)
  • Semiconductors — can act as either conductor or insulator depending on conditions; used in diodes and transistors
  • Insulators — electrons are tightly bound and cannot flow freely (rubber, plastic, glass)

Static electricity is a build-up of charge on an insulating surface. When enough charge accumulates, it can discharge as a spark — potentially damaging sensitive electronic components. Preventive measures include ESD (electrostatic discharge) wrist straps and proper grounding when handling electronics.

1.2 Voltage & Current

Voltage (symbol: V) is the electrical pressure that drives current through a circuit. It is measured in volts (V) — a 9 V battery provides 9 volts of pressure. Sources of voltage include batteries and generators. 1,000 V = 1 kV (kilovolt).

Voltage comes in two forms:

  • DC (Direct Current) — polarity is constant; current flows in one direction. Produced by batteries. Common values: 1.5 V, 3 V, 9 V, 12 V. Used in control systems and mechatronics.
  • AC (Alternating Current) — polarity reverses periodically. Used in household power outlets (120 V in the US).

Current (symbol: I) is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. It is measured in amperes (A). Current requires a closed circuit — a complete, unbroken loop from the voltage source, through the load, and back. An open circuit (broken path) stops all current flow.

Note: Conventional current flows from + to −, while electron flow is actually − to +. Both conventions are used in electronics.

1.3 Resistance

Resistance (symbol: R) is the opposition to the flow of current. It is measured in ohms (symbol: Ω). A resistor is a component specifically designed to limit current and control voltage levels in a circuit.

The resistance of a conductor depends on four factors:

  • Material — copper has lower resistance than iron
  • Length — longer wire → more resistance
  • Cross-sectional area — thicker wire → less resistance
  • Temperature — most metals increase in resistance as temperature rises

Common resistance units: 1 kΩ (kiloohm) = 1,000 Ω  |  1 MΩ (megaohm) = 1,000,000 Ω

The value of a resistor is encoded in color bands painted on its body. A standard 4-band resistor has three value bands and one tolerance band.

1.4 Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in a circuit. It states that current is directly proportional to voltage and inversely proportional to resistance:

Figure Figure 1.2 — Ohm's Law Triangle
V
I
R
V = I × R
I = V ÷ R
R = V ÷ I
Cover the unknown letter to reveal the formula for that quantity.
V = Voltage (volts)  |  I = Current (amps)  |  R = Resistance (ohms)  |  ↑V → ↑I  |  ↑R → ↓I

Power (symbol: P) measures the rate at which energy is used, measured in watts (W). Power in a DC circuit can be calculated three ways:

  • P = V × I — voltage times current
  • P = I² × R — current squared times resistance
  • P = V² ÷ R — voltage squared divided by resistance

Example: A microwave rated at 1,200 W on a 120 V outlet draws I = P ÷ V = 1200 ÷ 120 = 10 A of current.

1.5 Circuit Analysis

Resistors can be connected in two fundamental ways:

Series circuit — components are connected end-to-end in a single path:

  • Current is the same through every component
  • Voltage is divided across each resistor
  • Total resistance adds: Rt = R1 + R2 + R3 + …

Parallel circuit — components are connected across the same two nodes, providing multiple paths:

  • Voltage is the same across every branch
  • Current is divided among branches
  • Total resistance is less than any single branch: 1/Rt = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + …

Household wiring uses parallel circuits — one burnt bulb does not turn off the whole house.
