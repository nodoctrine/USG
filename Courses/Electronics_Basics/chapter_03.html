3.1 Diodes

A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor component that allows current to flow in only one direction. It is built from a P-N junction — a P-type semiconductor region (the anode) bonded to an N-type region (the cathode).

The schematic symbol is a triangle pointing toward a vertical bar. The base of the triangle is the anode; the bar is the cathode. Current flows in the direction the triangle points — from anode to cathode.

  • Forward bias — anode is more positive than cathode; current flows once V exceeds the forward voltage threshold
  • Reverse bias — cathode is more positive; current is blocked (ideally zero)
  • Forward voltage (Vf) — the voltage drop across a conducting diode; approximately 0.7 V for silicon, 0.3 V for germanium, 1.5–3.5 V for LEDs

Common diode types: 1N4007 (power rectifier, up to 1 A), 1N4148 (fast signal diode, low current), Zener (controlled reverse breakdown for voltage regulation), and LED (emits light when forward biased).

Lecture 3.1.1 — Diode I-V Characteristics ○
Step 1 — The P-N Junction

Inside a diode, P-type material (excess holes) meets N-type material (excess electrons) at a junction. A thin depletion region forms at the boundary, creating a built-in electric field.

P-type  |  N-type
(+holes)  |  (−electrons)
Anode    |  Cathode
This built-in field must be overcome before current can flow — which is what the forward voltage threshold represents.
Step 2 — Forward Bias

Apply a positive voltage to the anode relative to the cathode. When Vanode − Vcathode exceeds ~0.7 V:

  • The depletion region narrows
  • Current flows freely from anode to cathode
  • The diode voltage drop stays approximately constant at Vf ≈ 0.7 V

Circuit current: I = (VS − Vf) / R
Step 3 — Reverse Bias

Apply a positive voltage to the cathode instead. The depletion region widens, reinforcing the barrier.

  • Virtually no current flows (only a tiny leakage current)
  • The full reverse voltage appears across the diode
  • If Vreverse exceeds the breakdown voltage, the diode conducts destructively (unless it is a Zener, which is designed for this)

3.2 Diode Circuits & H-Bridge

An H-bridge is a circuit that uses four switches arranged in an H shape to control a DC motor in both directions. By closing different pairs of switches, current can flow through the motor in either direction, reversing its spin.

When a motor runs, the spinning coil is an inductor storing magnetic energy. If the driving current is suddenly interrupted (a switch opens), that energy must go somewhere — it appears as a large voltage spike called back EMF (electromotive force). This spike can easily exceed the transistor's breakdown voltage and destroy it.

  • Flyback diode (also called a freewheeling diode) — placed in parallel with the motor, reverse-biased during normal operation, but catches the back-EMF spike when a switch opens and conducts the current safely back to the supply
  • Each transistor switch in an H-bridge gets its own flyback diode, clamping spikes to approximately Vsupply + 0.7 V
  • Without protection, back EMF spikes can reach many times the supply voltage in microseconds

Lecture 3.2.1 — H-Bridge Operation & Back EMF ○
Step 1 — The H-Bridge Structure

Picture the letter H: the motor sits in the crossbar, two switches are on the left leg (S1 top, S2 bottom), two are on the right leg (S3 top, S4 bottom).

The top of both legs connects to V+. The bottom of both connects to ground. By choosing which pair to close, you choose which direction current flows through the motor.
Step 2 — Forward Direction

Close S1 (top-left) and S4 (bottom-right). Leave S2 and S3 open.

V+ → S1 → Motor → S4 → GND
Current flows left-to-right through the motor coil. The motor spins in the forward direction.
Step 3 — Reverse Direction

Now open S1/S4 and close S2 (bottom-left) and S3 (top-right).

V+ → S3 → Motor → S2 → GND
Current flows right-to-left — the motor reverses. The H-bridge gives full bidirectional control with no rewiring.
Step 4 — Back EMF Protection

When a switch opens, the motor coil (an inductor) fights the change in current:

  • L di/dt generates a large voltage spike — back EMF
  • Without protection this spike can be many times Vsupply
  • Flyback diodes are placed reverse-biased across each switch
  • When a spike occurs, the diode turns on and conducts the energy back to V+, clamping the voltage to Vsupply + 0.7 V
Four switches arranged in an H shape around a motor.

3.3 BJT Structure & Modes

A Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) is a three-terminal semiconductor device that uses a small base current to control a much larger collector current. It is a current-controlled device — unlike a FET, which is voltage-controlled.

An NPN BJT has three terminals: Emitter (E), Base (B), and Collector (C). Two PN junctions form inside — one between base and emitter, one between base and collector. The base region is very thin, which is why charge carriers injected through the B-E junction can cross and exit at the collector.

Key current relationships:

  • IC = β × IB — collector current equals current gain times base current
  • IE = IC + IB — emitter current is the sum of collector and base currents
  • β (also written hFE) is the DC current gain, typically 30–300 depending on the transistor and operating point
  • VBE ≈ 0.6–0.7 V is required to forward-bias the base-emitter junction and turn the transistor on

Two operating modes relevant to switching:

  • Cutoff — IB = 0, no base current, transistor is fully OFF (IC ≈ 0)
  • Saturation — IB is large enough to drive IC to its maximum; transistor is fully ON; VCE(sat) ≈ 0.1–0.2 V

Step 1 — Structure & Terminals

The NPN BJT consists of two N-type regions sandwiching a very thin P-type base region:

Collector (N)
⎯⎯ B-C junction ⎯⎯
Base (P) ← control input
⎯⎯ B-E junction ⎯⎯

A small current into the base controls a much larger current from collector to emitter.
Step 2 — Current Gain (β)

The base-emitter junction behaves like a diode. When VBE ≥ 0.7 V, base current IB flows, and by transistor action, a much larger IC flows:

IC = β × IB
Example: β = 100, IB = 1 mA → IC = 100 mA.

All current exits through the emitter: IE = IB + IC = 1 + 100 = 101 mA.
Step 3 — Cutoff Mode (Switch OFF)

When VBE < 0.7 V (e.g., base connected to ground), no base current flows:

  • IB = 0
  • IC ≈ 0 (only tiny leakage)
  • VCE ≈ Vsupply (full supply voltage across transistor)
  • Transistor acts like an open switch

This is the off state in a digital switching application.
Step 4 — Saturation Mode (Switch ON)

When IB is large enough that β × IB would exceed the circuit's available current, the transistor is saturated:

  • VCE(sat) ≈ 0.1–0.2 V (nearly a short circuit)
  • IC is set by the external circuit, not by β × IB
  • Transistor acts like a closed switch

Design rule: drive IB to at least IC/β to guarantee saturation.
The BJT has three terminals and two internal PN junctions.

3.4 BJT as a Switch

A BJT used as a digital switch operates only in cutoff (OFF) and saturation (ON). The low-side switch topology places the transistor between the load and ground — the load sits above the transistor, connected to V+, and the emitter connects to ground.

When a microcontroller GPIO pin drives the base high, the transistor saturates and the load sees nearly the full supply voltage. When the GPIO goes low, the transistor cuts off and the load is de-energised.

Design procedure — to choose RB:

  • Step 1: Find iC(sat) — the collector current when the transistor is fully on. For a simple resistive or LED load: iC(sat) = (V+ − VCE(sat) − Vload) / Rload
  • Step 2: Find the required base current: iB = iC(sat) / β (use the minimum guaranteed β from the datasheet for worst-case design)
  • Step 3: Find RB: RB = (Vin − VBE) / iB

The 2N3904 is a standard NPN BJT for this task: IC max 200 mA, β minimum ∼100 at 10 mA, VCE(sat) max 200 mV, VBE ≈ 0.6–0.7 V.

Lecture 3.4.1 — Design Procedure Walk-Through ○
Step 1 — Identify the Circuit

Example: A Raspberry Pi Pico GPIO pin (3.3 V) drives a 2N3904 to switch an LED circuit. Known values:

VGPIO3.3 V
V+ (motor/LED supply)5 V
Target ILED20 mA
β (2N3904 min)100
VBE0.6 V
Step 2 — Find iC(sat)

In saturation the transistor is fully on. The load (LED + series resistor) sets the collector current. We want the LED to carry 20 mA, so:

iC(sat) = 20 mA
(In a resistive-only load you would calculate: iC(sat) = (V+ − VCE(sat)) / Rload. Here we specified the target directly.)
Step 3 — Find Required iB

To guarantee saturation, we need:

iB = iC(sat) / β = 20 mA / 100 = 0.2 mA
Always use the minimum β from the datasheet (worst-case part). This ensures every unit of the transistor saturates, even low-gain specimens.
Step 4 — Find RB

The base resistor drops (VGPIO − VBE). With iB = 0.2 mA = 0.0002 A:

RB = (3.3 − 0.6) / 0.0002 = 2.7 / 0.0002 = 13 500 Ω
Choose the nearest standard resistor value below this (e.g. 12 kΩ) to keep sufficient base drive. The design is complete.
Identify the circuit topology and all known values before calculating.
