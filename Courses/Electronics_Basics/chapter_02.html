2.1 Electrical Power

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted. Every resistor, motor, and light bulb that draws current is consuming power. The SI unit of power is the watt (W), where 1 W = 1 joule per second.

The fundamental power formula combines voltage and current:

  • P = V × I — power equals voltage times current
  • By substituting Ohm's Law (V = I·R), two more forms follow
  • All three forms are equivalent — use whichever two quantities you already know

Energy is power multiplied by time: E = P × t (joules = watts × seconds). Your electricity bill measures energy in kilowatt-hours (kWh), where 1 kWh = 3.6 MJ.

Lecture 2.1.1 — Deriving the Power Formulas ○
Step 1 — The Base Formula

Power is defined as the rate of energy delivery:

P = V × I
A component with 5 V across it drawing 2 A consumes 10 W.
Units: volts × amperes = watts (V·A = W).
Step 2 — Eliminate Voltage (use I and R)

Ohm's Law says V = I × R. Substitute into P = V × I:

P = (I × R) × I = I² × R
Example: A 10 Ω resistor carrying 3 A dissipates P = 9 × 10 = 90 W.
Notice power grows with the square of current — double the current, quadruple the power.
Step 3 — Eliminate Current (use V and R)

Ohm's Law also gives I = V / R. Substitute into P = V × I:

P = V × (V / R) = V² / R
Example: 120 V across a 1440 Ω heater element: P = 14400 / 1440 = 10 W.
Power also grows with the square of voltage — useful when voltage is the known quantity.
Step 4 — Summary

KnownFormula
V and IP = V × I
I and RP = I² × R
V and RP = V² / R
Start with the definition: power is energy per unit time.

2.2 Kirchhoff's Current Law

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) states that the algebraic sum of all currents at any node in a circuit equals zero. More intuitively: the total current flowing into a node equals the total current flowing out.

A node is any point in a circuit where two or more conductors meet. KCL is a consequence of charge conservation — charge cannot accumulate at a node; every coulomb that arrives must leave.

  • Assign a sign convention: currents entering a node are positive, currents leaving are negative (or vice versa — just be consistent)
  • Write the equation: ΣIin = ΣIout
  • KCL applies at every node simultaneously — it is the basis for nodal analysis
Figure Figure 2.2 — KCL at a Node
I₁ = 3 A →
I₂ = 5 A → → I₃ = ?
KCL: I₁ + I₂ = I₃  →  3 + 5 = 8 A
The orange dot is the node. Currents in (blue) must equal current out (red).
Lecture 2.2.1 — Applying KCL Step by Step ○
Step 1 — Identify the Node

A node is where wires connect. It has no physical size — it's a mathematical point.

Think of a pipe junction: water flowing in from multiple pipes must all flow out somewhere. Current behaves the same way. The junction point is the node.
Step 2 — Why KCL Is True

Electrons carry charge. If more electrons arrived at a node than left, charge would accumulate there, building up a large negative field that would repel further current.

In a steady-state circuit this doesn't happen — the circuit self-balances so that ΣIin = ΣIout at every node.
Step 3 — Write the Equation

At a node with currents I₁, I₂ flowing in and I₃ flowing out:

I₁ + I₂ = I₃
If I₁ = 4 A and I₂ = 6 A, then I₃ = 10 A.

Always define current directions before applying KCL. If a value turns out negative, the actual current flows opposite to your assumed direction.
Step 4 — Multiple Nodes

Real circuits have many nodes. Apply KCL at each one independently:

  • Pick a node
  • List all currents entering and leaving
  • Write ΣIin = ΣIout
  • Solve for the unknown

Combined with KVL (next section), this gives you a complete system of equations to solve any circuit.
A node is any junction where conductors meet.

2.3 Kirchhoff's Voltage Law

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop in a circuit equals zero. Equivalently: the sum of voltage rises equals the sum of voltage drops around any loop.

This follows from energy conservation. A charge carrier travelling around a closed loop and returning to its starting point gains and loses exactly equal amounts of energy — net change is zero.

  • A voltage rise occurs when traversing a source from − to + (e.g., a battery)
  • A voltage drop occurs when traversing a resistor in the direction of current flow
  • Pick a loop, assign a direction (clockwise or counter-clockwise), and sum: ΣV = 0
Figure Figure 2.3 — KVL in a Series Loop
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│ + Vs = 12 V (source rise) │
│ ─────── I → ────────────── │
│ VR1 drop VR2 drop │
└─────────────────────────────┘
KVL: Vs − VR1 − VR2 = 0  →  Vs = VR1 + VR2
Traversing clockwise: source is a rise (+12 V), each resistor is a drop (−VR1, −VR2). Sum = 0.
Lecture 2.3.1 — Applying KVL Step by Step ○
Step 1 — Choose a Loop

A loop is any closed path through a circuit that returns to the starting point.

A simple series circuit (one battery, two resistors) has exactly one loop. Complex circuits have multiple loops — apply KVL to each one to generate equations.
Step 2 — Identify Rises and Drops

Travelling clockwise around the loop:
  • Battery (− to +): voltage rise → add to sum
  • Resistor (in current direction): voltage drop → subtract from sum

If you enter a component at the + terminal, it's a drop. If at −, it's a rise.
Step 3 — Write and Solve

Circuit: Vs = 9 V, R₁ = 3 Ω, R₂ = 6 Ω.

Find loop current I, then voltage across each resistor:
Rt = 3 + 6 = 9 Ω  →  I = 9 / 9 = 1 A
VR1 = 1 × 3 = 3 V
VR2 = 1 × 6 = 6 V
KVL check: 9 − 3 − 6 = 0 ✓
Step 4 — The Key Insight

KVL is a constraint, not a formula to "solve." It tells you that the supply voltage is shared among the components in a loop — each resistor gets a voltage proportional to its resistance (voltage divider behaviour).

VR = Vs × (R / Rtotal)
Larger resistance → larger share of the voltage.
Start by tracing a closed loop through the circuit.

2.4 Capacitors

A capacitor is a component that stores electrical charge. It consists of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called the dielectric. When voltage is applied, charge builds up on the plates — equal and opposite — creating an electric field across the dielectric.

Capacitance (symbol C) is the charge stored per unit voltage: Q = C × V. The SI unit is the farad (F), though practical capacitors are usually measured in microfarads (µF, 10⁻⁶ F) or picofarads (pF, 10⁻¹² F).

  • A capacitor blocks DC once fully charged — no steady-state current flows
  • A capacitor passes AC — alternating voltage keeps charging and discharging it
  • Energy stored: E = ½ × C × V² (joules)
  • Larger capacitance or higher voltage = more stored energy

Lecture 2.4.1 — How a Capacitor Works ○
Step 1 — Structure

A capacitor has two metal plates facing each other, separated by a non-conducting dielectric (air, ceramic, plastic film, or electrolyte).

(+) ──── | | ──── (−)
The dielectric prevents direct current from flowing through, but allows an electric field to form across it.
Step 2 — Charging

Connect a battery. Electrons flow from the negative terminal onto one plate (−), and are pulled away from the other plate (+).

  • Current flows freely at first (capacitor voltage = 0)
  • As charge builds up, the capacitor voltage rises
  • Current decreases as Vcap approaches Vbattery
Step 3 — Fully Charged

When Vcap = Vsource, there is no longer a voltage difference to drive current. Current drops to zero.

The capacitor now holds a charge Q = C × V and energy E = ½CV². It can hold this charge indefinitely (in an ideal capacitor) after the source is disconnected.

This is why capacitors block DC at steady state.
Step 4 — Discharging

Disconnect the battery and connect a resistor. The stored charge now flows through the resistor as current — opposite direction to charging.

  • Current is large initially (full voltage across resistor)
  • Decreases exponentially as Vcap falls
  • Time constant: τ = R × C (seconds)
  • Fully discharged after ~5τ
A capacitor is two conductive plates separated by an insulator.
